The `safeparts` CLI fits runbooks where recovery should require cooperation.
It does not replace a secrets manager, a vault, or access control.
It gives you a way to split one recovery secret into shares and require at least <var>k</var> shares during recovery.

This guide is written for teams that already operate production systems, incident procedures, and credential rotation.
It focuses on where the CLI belongs in those workflows.

:::caution
A share is sensitive. Do not paste real shares into tickets, chat, CI logs, screenshots, or incident notes.
Record custody and approvals, not share contents.
:::

## Where Safeparts helps

Safeparts is useful for secrets that should not sit in one administrator account or one vault item:

- break-glass cloud credentials
- password manager recovery keys
- release signing key material
- offline recovery tokens for core infrastructure
- emergency access for a small operations team

It is a poor fit for routine service-to-service secrets, high-frequency rotation, or secrets that need automatic online access.
Use your normal secrets manager for those.

## Operational model

A good Safeparts runbook answers five questions:

1. What secret is split?
2. Who holds each share?
3. How many shares are required?
4. Where does recovery happen?
5. What rotates after recovery?

The last question matters.
If an incident brings shares together, treat the recovered secret and the gathered shares as exposed.
Rotate the underlying credential and create a fresh split after the incident.

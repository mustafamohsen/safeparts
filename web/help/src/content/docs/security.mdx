Safeparts reduces single points of failure. It does not choose safe holders, protect your storage locations, or prevent an approved quorum from recovering the secret.

## What to assume

- Anyone with at least <var>k</var> valid shares from the same split can recover the secret.
- Fewer than <var>k</var> shares should not reveal useful information about the secret.
- If fewer than <var>k</var> shares remain available, recovery is impossible.
- A passphrase adds another requirement, but it does not fix a weak storage plan.

:::caution
Treat each share as sensitive. Do not paste real shares into chat, tickets, issues, CI logs, screenshots, or analytics tools.
:::

## Storage rules

<Steps>
1. Separate shares across independent people, accounts, devices, or physical locations.
2. Avoid keeping enough shares in one vault, safe, drive, ticket, repository, or CI secret store.
3. Keep a custody record that names the holder or role for each share without storing the share text.
4. Practice recovery with synthetic data before relying on the plan.
5. Rotate the underlying secret after any real recovery or suspected exposure.
</Steps>

## Passphrases

When you add a passphrase, Safeparts encrypts the secret before splitting it. Recovery then requires both enough shares and the passphrase.

Use a passphrase when shares may travel through higher-risk environments, when share holders should not be enough on their own, or when policy requires a knowledge factor.

Keep the passphrase separate from the shares. If the same person, vault, or device can provide both the passphrase and enough shares, the extra protection is mostly procedural.

## Local execution boundaries

The web app performs split and combine in your browser through WASM. The desktop app uses local Tauri commands over `safeparts_core`. The CLI and TUI run locally on your machine.

Safeparts does not upload secrets by default. Secrets can still leak if you paste them into another service, use a modified build, save them to an unsafe location, or let logs and screen recorders capture them.

## If something goes wrong

- If one share is lost, check whether at least <var>k</var> shares still remain. If yes, recover, rotate the underlying secret, and create a new split.
- If one share may have been copied, treat that share as compromised.
- If enough shares may have been copied to reach <var>k</var>, treat the original secret as compromised.
- If recovery happens on an untrusted machine, rotate the recovered credential as soon as possible.
